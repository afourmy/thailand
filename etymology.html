Etymology

Overview

The original Thai vocabulary. Before any outside influence, Thai had a stock of one-syllable, tonal words for everyday things: body parts (หัว, ตา, มือ), family (พ่อ, แม่, ลูก), basic actions (กิน, นอน, เดิน), small numbers, pronouns, and most short connecting words. Native Thai words never use the special letters that were added later for Indian borrowings (ฏ ฐ ฑ ฒ ณ, ศ, ษ, ฤ, ฬ) and never have a silent letter marked with การันต์.

Khmer and the Ayutthaya court. From the 13th to the 18th century, Khmer culture dominated the Thai court, first through the Angkor empire and then through the Ayutthaya kingdom that adopted its administration. This left a thick Khmer layer in royal vocabulary, government words, and many everyday two-syllable words.

Sanskrit and Pali. Two old Indian languages contributed an enormous amount of Thai vocabulary. Sanskrit is the source for formal, royal, and scientific words. Pali, the language of the Theravada Buddhist scriptures, is the source for religious words.

Chinese. Large-scale Teochew Chinese immigration in the 19th and early 20th centuries brought a small set of very common words, mostly for food (ก๋วยเตี๋ยว, เต้าหู้, ซาลาเปา, บะหมี่, ติ่มซำ), business (เถ้าแก่), and ways of addressing family members (เจ๊, อาเฮีย, อาม่า). Chinese loans tend to be short and use the rarer tone marks (ไม้ตรี อ๊ and ไม้จัตวา อ๋) much more often than other word groups.

The script tells you where a word comes from. Many features of Thai spelling exist not to reflect today's pronunciation but to mark a word's origin. A silent letter under การันต์, the letters ศ or ษ rather than ordinary ส, the special vowel ฤ, or a long stack of consonants: each of these is a sign of an Indian-origin word. The letter ฬ almost always marks a Pali word; ฤ, ศ, or ษ almost always mark a Sanskrit word.

Word parts to recognise. A few prefixes and suffixes appear over and over, and once you know them most long words decompose at a glance. From Sanskrit and Pali: -กรรม ("the act of"), -ภาพ ("the state of"), -ศาสตร์ ("the science of"), -วิทยา ("the study of"), -นิยม ("-ism"), and the prefix การ- ("the act of"). From Khmer: the small front syllables บัง-, บรร-, ประ-, กระ-.

Origin sets the level of formality. The source language of a word usually decides how formal it feels. The five Thai words for "to eat" make this clear: กิน (native Thai, neutral or casual), ทาน (Pali, polite), รับประทาน (Sanskrit and Khmer combined, formal), เสวย (Khmer, for royalty), ฉัน (Pali, for monks). They are not interchangeable. Etymology is what organises modern Thai vocabulary into these distinct levels.

Historical waves

Native Thai words. The native layer was already in place before any outside contact. Inherited from Proto-Tai (the ancestor of the Tai language family), it forms the basic everyday core of Thai.

Mon and the Dvaravati period. The Dvaravati kingdoms ruled the central plain from roughly the 6th to the 11th century. They spoke Mon, an Austroasiatic language (the same family as Khmer). Mon priests and monks were the first to bring Theravada Buddhism into the area, before Khmer power took over. Few Mon words can still be clearly identified in modern Thai, partly because the later Khmer layer covered them.

Khmer and the Ayutthaya court. The Khmer empire, centred at Angkor, was the dominant power in mainland Southeast Asia from roughly the 9th to the 13th century. When Ayutthaya was founded in 1351, the Thai court took over Khmer administrative culture, including a great deal of vocabulary and many spelling conventions. The result is that almost the entire formal-but-not-Indian register of Thai is Khmer. Borrowing happened in two waves: older everyday vocabulary that became fully naturalised, and later court-period vocabulary that often kept a royal or government meaning.

Sanskrit and Pali. Sanskrit is the classical language of ancient India: the language of Hindu religion, of royal courts, and of scholarship. Pali is a slightly later sister language, used as the scripture language of Theravada Buddhism. The two are closely related, so many roots appear in both, just in slightly different shapes. Sanskrit feeds Thai's vocabulary for government, royalty, science, art, and Hindu religion; Pali feeds vocabulary for Buddhism and for several everyday words with a religious origin. These words reached Thai through several routes: directly from Indian Brahmin priests at court; indirectly through Khmer (which had already adopted them); through Mon during the Dvaravati period (for the earliest religious terms); and through Sri Lanka by way of Buddhist monk lineages (especially for Pali). The Sanskrit and Pali layer is the single largest source of long Thai words.

Chinese. Before the modern era, Chinese borrowings in Thai were few. The big Chinese layer arrived with mass Teochew immigration starting in the late 18th century and peaking in the 19th and early 20th. Teochew immigrants settled in Bangkok and the central plain as merchants, so Teochew became the dominant Chinese variety in Thailand. Smaller contributions came from Hokkien, Hakka, and Cantonese. Loans concentrate in food, business, and family terms.

European contact. Portugal opened formal contact with Ayutthaya in 1511. The early European layer is small but lasting: ปัง (bread, from Portuguese pão), สบู่ (soap, from Portuguese sabão). The Dutch, French, and English traders who followed left little behind directly. The 19th century brought heavier European contact, and during the French Indochina period (roughly 1860 to 1954) a handful of French words entered. By the late 20th century, English had become the dominant source for new loanwords, covering technology (คอมพิวเตอร์), business (มาร์เก็ตติ้ง), and youth culture.

Recognition by source language

How to identify a word's source from its written form alone. Some clues are guarantees (the letter ฤ means Sanskrit, no exceptions); others only suggest a likely source.

Native Thai words

Three checks together:

  1. The word is one syllable on its own.
  2. It uses none of the borrowed letters (ฤ, ฦ, ศ, ษ, ฬ, ฎ, ฏ, ฐ, ฑ, ฒ, ณ).
  3. It has no silent letter (no การันต์).

A word that passes all three is almost certainly native. A word that fails any one is almost certainly borrowed. The most common exception is a compound built from native pieces (ปลายเท้า "tiptoe"), which has more than one syllable but each piece is still native. The final consonant of a native word is one of the eight standard Thai final sounds.

The meaning helps too: the native layer covers the most basic everyday domains.

  • Body parts: ตา (eye), หู (ear), ปาก (mouth), มือ (hand), เท้า (foot), ขา (leg), ใจ (heart), ท้อง (belly).
  • Family: พ่อ (father), แม่ (mother), ลูก (child), พี่ (older sibling), น้อง (younger sibling), ปู่, ย่า, ตา, ยาย.
  • Basic actions and states: ไป (go), มา (come), อยู่ (be at), นั่ง (sit), นอน (lie down), กิน (eat), ดื่ม (drink), ดู (look), ฟัง (listen).
  • Small numbers: หนึ่ง through สิบ are all native.
  • Pronouns and short connecting words: กู, มึง, เรา, เขา, มัน, and most particles. The polite "I" word ฉัน looks short and native but is actually a Pali borrowing.

Khmer words

The two strongest clues are the small front syllable and the silent-leading-consonant pattern.

Recognisable front syllables: บัง-, บรร-, ประ-, กระ-, ผ-, ตะ-.

The silent-leading-consonant pattern (อักษรนำ): the word is written with two consonants in a row, but the first is silent and only changes the tone class of the second. สนุก looks like it should start with "sa-nu-k" but it is actually pronounced "sa-nuk" with a high-class tone on น, because the silent ส raised its class. Many naturalised everyday Khmer words use this pattern: ขนม (sweet), สนุก (fun), สบาย (comfortable).

A few short Khmer words form the core of elevated or royal vocabulary: เสด็จ (royal "go"), ทรง (royal verb-prefix), บรรทม (royal "sleep"), ราชดำเนิน (royal procession, a hybrid with the Sanskrit ราช "royal").

Sanskrit and Pali words

Indian-origin words are typically longer than one syllable and tend to show one or more of:

  • A long stack of consonants without a vowel between them.
  • A silent letter at the end, marked by การันต์: ภูมิ์, สิทธิ์, สัตว์.
  • One of the special consonants ฎ, ฏ, ฐ, ฑ, ฒ, ณ.
  • One of the four Sanskrit-only letters: ฤ, ฦ, ศ, ษ. Any of these is a guarantee of Sanskrit origin.
  • The letter ฬ: a guarantee of Pali origin.
  • Characteristic endings: -ตา, -ภาพ, -ศาสตร์, -วิทยา, -ธรรม, -นิยม, -กรรม.

If a word contains any of ฎ ฏ ฐ ฑ ฒ ณ or ends in a silent letter under การันต์, it is almost certainly Indian in origin.

Chinese words

Chinese loans are typically one or two syllables, rarely three. They often carry the tone marks ไม้ตรี อ๊ and ไม้จัตวา อ๋, uncommon in native Thai and almost absent in Indian borrowings. They belong to food, business, family address, or related cultural areas.

Examples by domain:

  • Food: ก๋วยเตี๋ยว (rice noodles), เต้าหู้ (tofu), ซาลาเปา (bao bun), บะหมี่ (egg noodles), ติ่มซำ (dim sum), หมั่นโถว (mantou), จับฉ่าย (mixed vegetable stew), เก๊กฮวย (chrysanthemum drink).
  • Business and trade: เถ้าแก่ (boss), เซียน (expert), ฮวงจุ้ย (feng shui).
  • Family terms: อาม่า (grandmother), อากง (grandfather), อาเฮีย (older brother), เจ๊ (older sister).

European words

European loans are spelled by sounding out the original word in Thai letters. They often use การันต์ on a final consonant that Thai cannot pronounce normally (ฟิล์ม, คอนเสิร์ต, เบียร์ from beer). The shape of the word often breaks ordinary Thai syllable rules, which itself is a clue. Tone marks are added to imitate English stress and pitch, not to mark inherited tones.

Sanskrit versus Pali

Sanskrit and Pali are close relatives, so the same ancient word often gave Thai two slightly different forms, one through each. Telling them apart is the single most useful piece of etymological knowledge in Thai, because doublets are everywhere and the two members feel different (one usually formal, one usually religious or homely).

How they differ

Pali simplifies what Sanskrit complicates. Two patterns are visible in Thai spelling.

Consonant stacks become doubled letters. Where Sanskrit stacks two different consonants together, Pali doubles one of them. Sanskrit ปรัชญา ("philosophy") has the stack ชญ; the Pali version smooths it to ปัญญา with a doubled ญ. Sanskrit อาตมา (with the stack ตม) becomes Pali อัตตา (with doubled ตต).

Some letters appear only in one of the two. Sanskrit's three letters for "s" sounds (ศ ษ ส) collapse to a single ส in Pali: Sanskrit ศานติ (peace) becomes Pali สันติ; Sanskrit โทษ (fault) becomes Pali โทส. The vowel letters ฤ, ฦ, ฤๅ are Sanskrit-only. The letter ฬ is Pali-only.

  • Sanskrit only: ฤ, ฦ, ฤๅ, ศ, ษ. Examples: ฤดู (season), ฤกษ์ (auspicious moment), พฤหัสบดี (Thursday), ฤๅษี (sage), ศาสตร์ (science), ประเทศ (country), ศิลปะ (art), กษัตริย์ (king), โทษ (fault), มนุษย์ (human).
  • Pali only: ฬ. Examples: กีฬา (sport), จุฬา (topknot, crown, as in Chulalongkorn).
  • Either: ส, ฌ, ญ, ฎ, ฏ, ฐ, ฑ, ฒ, ณ, ธ, ภ. Check the rest of the word.

Put together: if you see two similar Thai words, the one with the longer consonant stack, or with ฤ, ศ, or ษ, is the Sanskrit form; the smoother one is the Pali form.

Word-building blocks

Once a small set of prefixes and suffixes is internalised, most long Thai words can be broken down at a glance. The two productive sources are Khmer (front syllables) and Sanskrit / Pali (endings).

Khmer front syllables

บัง-. Often makes a word causative (makes-something-happen) or intensive. บังคับ (force), บังเกิด (cause to arise), บังเอิญ (by chance, happen-to).

บรร-. A variant of บัง- used before certain consonants. บรรจุ (contain), บรรทุก (carry), บรรลุ (attain).

ประ-. A general-purpose front syllable. ประกอบ (compose), ประจำ (regular, stationed), ประจักษ์ (evident).

กระ-. Often verbal. กระทำ (do, perform), กระทบ (strike), กระจาย (scatter).

ผ-. ผสม (mix), ผจญ (encounter, confront).

These front syllables are unstressed and carry a short reduced vowel. Their effect is usually to turn a verb into a transitive or causative version.

Sanskrit and Pali endings

-กรรม ("the act of", from Sanskrit karman). Forms abstract nouns of action. อุตสาหกรรม (industry), เกษตรกรรม (agriculture), วรรณกรรม (literature), พาณิชยกรรม (commerce). The English equivalents end in -ing or -ation.

-ภาพ ("the state of", from Sanskrit bhāva). Forms abstract nouns of quality. มิตรภาพ (friendship), สถานภาพ (status), สุขภาพ (health), เสรีภาพ (freedom). The English equivalents end in -ness or -ship.

-ศาสตร์ ("the science of", from Sanskrit shastra). คณิตศาสตร์ (mathematics), เศรษฐศาสตร์ (economics), รัฐศาสตร์ (political science), ภาษาศาสตร์ (linguistics). The English equivalent is -ology or -ics.

-วิทยา ("the study of", from Sanskrit vidya). ชีววิทยา (biology), จิตวิทยา (psychology), สังคมวิทยา (sociology), ธรณีวิทยา (geology). Also -ology.

-ธรรม ("the way", "the dharma"). วัฒนธรรม (culture), อารยธรรม (civilisation).

-นิยม ("-ism", from Sanskrit niyama "rule"). ทุนนิยม (capitalism), สังคมนิยม (socialism), ชาตินิยม (nationalism), เสรีนิยม (liberalism).

การ- ("the act of", as a prefix). Placed before any verb to turn it into a noun meaning "the act of (verb)-ing". การกิน (eating), การเดิน (walking), การประชุม (the meeting), การศึกษา (education).

How compounds work

Indian-style compounds in Thai follow two simple patterns:

  • The first word qualifies or restricts the second. ราชวงศ์ "royal lineage" = ราช (royal) + วงศ์ (lineage). ธรรมศาสตร์ "law" = ธรรม + ศาสตร์ ("science of law").
  • The first word is an adjective describing the second. มหาราช "great king" = มหา (great) + ราช (king).

Thai also mixes layers freely in compounds. ทุนนิยม "capitalism" combines a native Thai word ทุน (capital) with a Sanskrit ending นิยม. โรงเรียน "school" combines two native words: โรง (building) and เรียน (learn).

Formality and meaning by source

Where a word comes from usually determines how formal it feels and which situation it belongs to.

Royal language (ราชาศัพท์)

A separate vocabulary used when speaking about or to the royal family. Most royal words are Khmer or Sanskrit; the system as a whole comes down from the Ayutthaya court. Royal verbs are built in three ways:

  • Put ทรง- in front of an ordinary verb: ทรงรับ (royal "to receive").
  • Put พระ- in front of a noun: พระหัตถ์ (royal "hand"), พระเนตร (royal "eye").
  • Use a special royal verb that replaces the ordinary one: เสด็จ (go), บรรทม (sleep), เสวย (eat), ตรัส (speak), ประชวร (be ill).

There are three different levels: one for the king and queen, one for high royalty, and one for monks. The monk-level vocabulary is built mainly on Pali words.

Religious vocabulary

Theravada Buddhist vocabulary is mostly Pali: ปัญญา (wisdom), ศีล (precept), สมาธิ (concentration), เมตตา (loving-kindness), ขันธ์ (aggregate), นิพพาน (nirvana). The one striking Sanskrit word in this otherwise Pali register is อาตมา, the monk's word for "I", preserved from the older Brahmin-influenced past. The verb a monk uses for "eat" is ฉัน, also Pali.

Hindu and Brahmin ritual vocabulary, separately, is Sanskrit: พิธีบรมราชาภิเษก (coronation rite), ฤกษ์ (auspicious moment), พรหม (Brahmā), ครุฑ (Garuda).

Academic and technical vocabulary

Modern technical Thai is built on Sanskrit roots. The Royal Society of Thailand coins new terms using -ศาสตร์, -วิทยา, -ภาพ, -กรรม, and -นิยม. English-origin words also enter this vocabulary, but they are usually transliterated (spelled out in Thai letters) rather than translated, for example คอมพิวเตอร์ "computer".

Everyday speech

Daily speech uses native Thai roots for verbs (กิน eat, ดู look, ฟัง listen), Chinese roots for food (ก๋วยเตี๋ยว, เต้าหู้), and English roots for modern objects (แท็กซี่, แอร์).

The clearest example of how etymology controls formality is the verb "to eat":

  • กิน: native Thai, neutral or casual.
  • ทาน: Pali, polite (a short form of รับประทาน).
  • รับประทาน: a Sanskrit-Khmer compound, formal polite.
  • ฉัน: Pali, used by monks.
  • เสวย: Khmer, used for royalty.

All five mean "to eat"; none of them can replace another.

Other common cases follow the same pattern.

To die:

  • ตาย: native Thai, neutral.
  • เสียชีวิต: native เสีย "lose" + Sanskrit ชีวิต "life", the standard polite term.
  • ถึงแก่กรรม: built on Sanskrit กรรม "action, end", more formal, common in obituaries.
  • มรณภาพ: Pali (มรณ "death" + ภาพ "state"), used for monks.
  • สวรรคต: Pali (สวรรค "heaven" + คต "gone"), used for kings and queens.

To sleep:

  • นอน: native Thai, neutral.
  • บรรทม: Khmer, used for royalty.
  • จำวัด: used for monks.

Head:

  • หัว: native Thai, neutral or colloquial.
  • ศีรษะ: Sanskrit, formal or medical.
  • พระเศียร: royal (พระ- + Sanskrit เศียร "head").
